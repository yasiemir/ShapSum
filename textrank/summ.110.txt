World leaders sent Japan messages of condolences after Emperor Hirohito's death, but   painful memories of World War II still generated criticism of the Japanese monarch.
World leaders expressed condolences at the death of Japan's 87-year-old Emperor   Hirohito, who surrendered his country at the end of World War II, but went on to preside   over its economic rise.
An opinion piece in the Los Angeles Times, referred to the death of Emperor Hirohito as   finishing the list of political and military leaders of the World War II leaders, one of the   darkest and bloodiest eras for both Asia and Europe.
To the rest of the world, he was known as Emperor Hirohito, but in Japan his name was   rarely used.
A British Broadcasting documentary claims that U.S. prosecutors pressured Japan's   wartime prime minister into changing testimony at his trial to avoid incriminating   Emperor Hirohito in war crimes.
Emperor Hirohito of Japan has lost 40 pounds in the two months since he fell ill, but his   internal bleeding appears to have stopped.
In 1945,   after the U.S. dropped atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki, Hirohito spoke out   against continuing the war and in a radio address to the nation announced Japan's   surrender.
